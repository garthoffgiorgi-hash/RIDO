# RIDO — Brand Guide (v0)

*A working source of truth. Bring this into Claude Design as context so every asset carries the right personality. Built from Giorgi's brand calibration; meant to be edited as RIDO sharpens.*

---

## 1. In one line

**RIDO is the rideshare for drivers who want to be paid what they deserve, and riders who want a fairer price — and to be part of changing how this works.**

The spoken hook is the name itself: *"Get a rido."* A play on "ride," built to become a verb between friends.

---

## 2. What we believe (the why underneath everything)

Uber and Lyft became predatory monopolies that take more from drivers every year while hiding it behind opaque pricing. That's one face of a bigger problem — work that no longer sustains the people doing it, and corporate margins that grow by squeezing them. RIDO exists to prove a ride can be cheaper for the rider *and* fairer to the driver at the same time, because the only thing standing in the way is how much the middle takes.

This belief is the **soul** of the brand. It is not the headline (see §4). It's the reason the headline is true, the thing that earns loyalty, and the reason a rider tells a friend "no — use RIDO."

**One discipline this demands:** a fairness brand makes its promise a constraint. Drivers and riders will hold RIDO to "fair." Build the economics so the promise stays keepable; never claim a fairness we can't fund.

---

## 3. Who it's for

**Riders** — the first wedge is the UCSD nightlife crowd (getting to and from parties, the 2am ride home), but the app is for any ride, any reason. They choose on price, safety, and reliability; the mission is what makes them stay and evangelize.

**Drivers** — students driving themselves, and local Uber/Lyft drivers already fed up with the take. (Interviews confirm: some would pay a $150–200/month fee and still come out ahead and prefer it.) Drivers are the supply that makes the marketplace work, so the brand treats them as the hero it's fighting *for*, not just labor it's hiring.

---

## 4. Message hierarchy (the most important page)

Three true things, stacked by altitude. Lead with the top; let the bottom resonate.

1. **Tangible (front and center):** cheaper, safe, fast, sleek. This is what converts the rider who just wants a ride home.
2. **Fair (right behind it):** drivers keep far more; you're paying an honest price, not a hidden markup.
3. **Movement (the soul, surfaced for those who lean in):** a stand against the squeeze — corporate greed is the villain.

Put #3 in the hero and we read preachy to a 19-year-old at 2am. Keep #3 as the reason behind #1 and #2 and it makes the whole thing stick. Marketing leads tangible; the about page, the driver pitch, and the moments people *choose* to read deeper are where the movement lives loud.

## 5. Voice & personality

**Personality:** efficient, kind, trustworthy, grounded. Warm enough to open people up; serious enough to actually help. Confident, never corporate.

**The rule that resolves "irreverent + trustworthy":**
- **Irreverence points outward — at the incumbents.** Punchy, a little cheeky, anti-monopoly. ("The other apps take up to half. We don't.")
- **Warmth and steadiness point inward — at the rider and driver.** Calm, clear, dependable. People are getting into cars with strangers at night; the voice toward *them* is solid and reassuring, never a smartass.

**Voice principles**
- Plain verbs, sentence case, no filler. Specific beats clever.
- Say what a thing does, not how it's built. "Driver keeps 87%," not "optimized payout architecture."
- Active voice. A button says exactly what happens: "Get a rido," "Accept," "Cancel."
- Confidence without arrogance. State the fairness as fact; don't lecture.
- Errors don't apologize or go vague — they say what happened and how to fix it.

**Do**
- "Get a rido." / "Cheaper for you. Fair for your driver."
- "Drivers keep 87% of every fare." (numbers, concrete)
- "The big apps quietly take up to half. We built the opposite." (irreverence aimed outward)

**Don't**
- Don't moralize at the rider ("Do the ethical thing"). Show the better deal; let them feel good choosing it.
- Don't oversell ("revolutionary," "disrupting mobility"). Be specific instead.
- Don't be cute about safety, payments, or driver pay. Those are warm-and-solid, always.

**Tagline system**
- Spoken hook: **"Get a rido."**
- Descriptor (pick per context): *The fair way to move.* / *Cheaper for you, fair for your driver.* / *Rideshare that pays drivers right.*

## 6. Visual direction

**Feel:** light UI, sleek and modern like the best rideshare apps — but a distinct RIDO layout, not a clone. The brand is anti-Uber; it must not look like Uber.

### Palette (tokens)
| Token | Hex | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Midnight | `#0B2A5B` | Primary brand. Headers, primary buttons, map pins, wordmark. |
| Signal | `#2A5BFF` | The single accent. Actions, links, live/active states. |
| Ivory | `#F7F5EF` | App canvas / background. |
| White | `#FFFFFF` | Cards, sheets, surfaces. |
| Mist | `#E7E3DA` | Dividers, hairlines, borders. |
| Ink | `#14171F` | Body text. (Slate `#5B5F69` for secondary text.) |

*Optional humanizer, use sparingly if blue ever feels cold:* a single warm gold `#E8A33D` for illustration/accents only — not core. Hold the discipline: two blues + ivory/white/mist + ink.

### Typography
- **Display / wordmark:** Sora — geometric, confident, modern. Used with restraint (headlines, big moments).
- **Body / UI:** Plus Jakarta Sans — clean, highly legible at small sizes; the workhorse for every label and button.
- **Numerals:** tabular figures everywhere a number appears — fares, ETAs, distances, counts. Non-negotiable for a money app.
- *(These are strong, freely-licensable starting faces. Swap to a licensed pairing later if RIDO wants more ownership — keep the same qualities: geometric-confident display + neutral-legible UI sans.)*

### Logo
- **Wordmark-forward.** RIDO is a word people say ("get a rido"), so the name carries the mark — no separate symbol needed at launch.
- Lowercase **rido** as primary (warm, modern); the **i** is the single accent in Signal blue — refine to just the tittle/dot in Design. Uppercase **RIDO** as the alt for contexts needing more authority.
- **Avoid the clichés:** no wheel, no location pin, no road/speed lines, no speedometer. The accent does the talking.

### UI principles
- Light UI on ivory/white; Midnight for structure and primary actions; Signal for interactive/live moments.
- Generous whitespace, large tap targets, medium corner radius (not bubbly).
- Brand-blue map markers — deliberately *not* the default red pin.
- Tabular numerals on every fare, ETA, and count.

---

## 7. What to avoid
- **Don't look like Uber/Lyft.** Borrow the polish, not the design.
- **Don't lead with the politics.** Movement is the soul, not the headline (§4).
- **Don't over-claim fairness** you can't fund — the promise is a constraint.
- **No Triton / UCSD-mascot references.** The campus is the beachhead, not the brand.
- **No rideshare clichés** in the mark (wheel, pin, speed lines).
